Organizational Silence
A Barrier to Change & Development in a Pluralistic World

✍️ Elizabeth Wolfe Morrison & Frances J. Milliken · NYU Stern
📖 Academy of Management Review — FT50 Journal

What happens when everyone knows the truth but nobody says a word? This landmark 2000 paper names it, maps it, and reveals why silence can quietly destroy organizations from the inside out. 🔇

🗺️ The Big Picture

Imagine an organization where the CEO has no clothes. Everyone can see it. But no one ever mentions this — except behind closed doors, in hushed whispers.

— Morrison & Milliken, 2000 (paraphrased opening)
🤫 What is Organizational Silence?

It's not just one quiet employee. It's a collective phenomenon — when employees en masse choose to withhold information, opinions, and concerns about real problems in their organization.

Think of it as an unwritten rule that spreads through a workplace: "Speaking up is not worth it here."

🔕 Collective 🏢 Organizational-level ⚠️ Systemic 🌍 Context-driven
🆚 Silence vs. Individual Choice

Prior research focused on why one person speaks up or stays quiet. This paper is different — it asks why whole organizations learn to stay silent.

The forces that make one brave soul speak up are completely different from the forces that keep the rest silent. 🔑

🔍 Where Does Silence Come From?

😰 Root Cause #1 — Managers Fear Negative Feedback

Top managers often feel threatened by bad news — especially from below. Feedback from subordinates is seen as less credible and more threatening to their power and authority.

So, consciously or not, they send signals: "Don't bring me problems." And the organization learns to comply. 🤐

🧠 Root Cause #2 — Three Dangerous Implicit Beliefs
  • 💰
    Belief 1: "Employees are self-interested and untrustworthy." — Rooted in economic Theory X thinking. Managers act on this belief without even realizing it. Employees sense this distrust and stop sharing.
  • 👑
    Belief 2: "Management knows best." — A top-down assumption that those in power have all the answers, and those below just need to execute. Input from below is seen as irrelevant or threatening.
  • 🤝
    Belief 3: "Unity and agreement are healthy; dissent is dangerous." — The unitary view of organizations. Disagreement is seen as a problem to be managed, not a resource to be used. Innovation dies here. 💀

🏗️ Structural Consequences of These Beliefs

🔒 What Gets Built?

These beliefs don't just stay in managers' heads — they shape the physical architecture of organizations:

  • 🏛️
    Centralized decision-making: Real authority stays at the top. Task forces and committees exist for show. Participation is theatre, not reality.
  • 🚫
    No upward feedback channels: Surveys, suggestion boxes, and town halls are either missing or performative. Why ask if you don't want to hear the answer?
  • 😠
    Hostile reactions to input: Managers visibly discount, dismiss, or punish messengers of bad news — even unconsciously. The "MUM Effect" in action.
  • 👀
    No informal feedback-seeking: Managers only talk to people who'll confirm what they already think. Confirmation bias at scale. 🔄
🌐 Who Is Most at Risk? Contextual Factors
📊
Finance-Heavy Top Teams
Orgs led by finance/economics-trained executives are more likely to hold Theory X beliefs about employees.
⏳
Long-Tenured Management
The longer the same team is together, the more entrenched and unquestioned their shared assumptions become.
🗼
Tall Hierarchies
Many organizational levels mean top managers are far removed from lower-level reality — and trust plummets.
🏭
Stable, Mature Industries
In low-velocity environments, there's less pressure to listen. Volatile industries need employee ideas to survive.
👥
Demographic Dissimilarity
When top management looks nothing like the broader workforce, distrust and silence deepen on both sides.
🔗
Contingent/Temp Workers
Temporary staff are seen as especially self-interested and untrustworthy — further tightening control and silence.

📉 Organizational Silence

This isn't a one-time event — it's a self-reinforcing loop. Silence validates the manager's fear. Fear strengthens the structure. The structure deepens the climate. 🔁

🌫️ The Climate of Silence

🤔 What Makes a "Climate"?

A climate of silence is a set of shared beliefs among employees — not individual fear, but a group understanding that:

Belief A"Speaking up is futile here."
Belief B"Voicing concerns is dangerous."

These beliefs don't just exist in people's private thoughts — they spread through collective sensemaking.

🕸️ How Does the Climate Spread? Collective Sensemaking

Employees watch each other, share stories, and collectively interpret the signals management sends. This is called collective sensemaking — social meaning-making in real time.

  • 👫
    Similarity: Homogeneous workgroups converge on shared perceptions faster — social contagion is stronger.
  • 🏠
    Stability: Stable workforces have more time to talk, compare notes, and solidify shared beliefs.
  • 🔗
    Interdependence: When people must work together closely, they communicate more — and shared perceptions form faster.
  • 🕸️
    Dense social networks: Strong, dense informal networks spread information (and fear) from person to person at speed.
⚠️ The Sensemaking Goes Wrong — Distortions

Here's the scary part: the collective sensemaking process systematically distorts reality toward more fear, not less.

  • 📡
    Secondhand signals: People learn about the risks of speaking up vicariously — from colleagues' stories — not firsthand. These secondhand accounts tend to exaggerate danger.
  • 🔗
    False connections: If someone speaks up and then gets passed over for promotion, people link the two events — even if they're unrelated. The grapevine does the rest.
  • 📣
    Overgeneralization: Silence about specific issues spreads to everything. Soon no one shares anything — even mildly positive suggestions.
  • 🔄
    Self-perpetuating: Seeing others stay silent reinforces the belief that silence is the only safe choice. A perfect loop. 🔁

⚡ What Does Silence Actually Do?

🧩 Two Types of Damage
🏢
Organizational Damage
Corrupts decision-making, blocks change, prevents learning, and turns diversity from an asset into a liability.
🧍
Human Damage
Causes employees to feel unvalued, powerless, and stuck — leading to disengagement, stress, and even sabotage.

🏢 Organizational-Level Consequences

🎯
Poor Decision Quality
Without multiple perspectives, decisions are made on incomplete information. Bad ideas don't get challenged. Great ideas never surface. 🪣
🚧
Change is Blocked
Change requires honest assessment of what's broken. Silence means the organization can't detect or correct its own errors. It drifts — and drifts further. 📉
🚫
No Double-Loop Learning
Argyris's concept: organizations can't question their own underlying assumptions if no one surfaces problems. They keep doing the wrong things, just more efficiently. 🔁
🌍
Pluralism Becomes Hollow
You can hire the most diverse team in the world — but if no one feels safe speaking their truth, diversity is just for show. 🎭
🧐
False Consensus at the Top
Managers interpret silence as agreement. They believe things are fine — and even brag about team alignment — while real problems compound in the shadows. 👤

🧍 Human-Level Consequences

💔
Feeling Unvalued
When you can't speak, you feel invisible. Perceived organizational support drops. Commitment and trust erode. People start mentally checking out. 🪄
🎮
Loss of Control
Human beings need agency. When voice is denied, the sense of control vanishes — leading to stress, withdrawal, and in some cases, destructive behaviour. 💥
🧠
Cognitive Dissonance
Knowing the truth but being unable to say it creates a painful internal conflict. You can't change your belief. You can't change your behaviour. You just... suffer. 😶
🎭
Worse for Marginalized Groups
Employees from underrepresented backgrounds feel these effects most acutely. Silence magnifies existing inequity — making organizational discrimination systemic, even if unintentional. ⚠️

"If you experience fear every day, it drags you down and you become cowardly. After my suggestions were ignored, the quality of my work was still there, but I wasn't."

— Employee quoted by Ryan & Oestreich (1991), cited in Morrison & Milliken (2000)

💡 The 13 Propositions

Morrison and Milliken offer 13 testable propositions — here they are, decoded in plain language: 📋

📌 Proposition 1
Silence is more likely when top management is dominated by people with economics or finance training — they hold stronger Theory X beliefs about workers.
📌 Proposition 2
The longer the same top team has been together, the stronger their shared silence-creating beliefs become.
📌 Proposition 3
High power distance + collectivistic cultures in top management = stronger belief that "the boss knows best" and dissent should be suppressed.
📌 Proposition 4
The greater the demographic gap between top management and employees below, the more distrust — and the more silence.
📌 Proposition 5
Orgs focused heavily on cost control or operating in scarce resource environments are more likely to punish dissent and value compliance.
📌 Proposition 6
Stable, mature industries breed more silence. Dynamic environments need employee ideas to survive — so they're forced to listen.
📌 Proposition 7
Tall hierarchies (many levels) and bringing in outside senior hires create distance between top and bottom — fueling distrust and silence.
📌 Proposition 8
Heavy use of contingent/temp workers deepens beliefs that employees are self-interested and unreliable.
📌 Proposition 9
Those beliefs lead to centralized decision-making and the removal of upward feedback channels.
📌 Proposition 10
Those beliefs also lead managers to reject employee input and stop seeking informal feedback from below.
📌 Proposition 11
Structural silence-creators are most powerful when employees interact closely among themselves — enabling fast social contagion of the silence norm.
📌 Proposition 12
Interaction among employees is amplified by: similarity, workforce stability, workflow interdependence, and dense/strong informal networks.
📌 Proposition 13
A climate of silence is stronger when there's high demographic dissimilarity between top management and the people they lead.

🎯 Why Does This Paper Still Matter?

📅 Written in 2000. Relevant in 2026.

This paper was written before Slack, before DEI strategies became standard, before "psychological safety" appeared in every HR deck, before the Great Resignation exposed just how many people had been quietly leaving — emotionally — for years before they physically walked out the door.

Morrison and Milliken saw all of this coming. Or rather — they saw it already there, hiding in plain sight. 👁️

⚠️ The Hidden Trap — Why Silence Persists
  • 🕵️
    The dynamics that create silence are invisible. Nobody calls it "organizational silence." It just feels like "how things are around here."
  • 🔄
    These cycles are self-reinforcing. Once trust is broken, it's nearly impossible to rebuild — employees will be cynical about any attempt to change the culture.
  • 🔝
    Breaking silence may require top management change — and possibly significant turnover at multiple levels throughout the organization.
  • 😶
    Even the fact that silence exists is often undiscussable. The silence is silenced. 🤯
🌍 And in a Pluralistic World?

The paper makes a powerful argument: diversity without voice is decoration. A pluralistic organization — one that truly values different perspectives, backgrounds, and experiences — simply cannot function if the system is designed to silence those differences.

You can hire the most diverse team on earth. But if the climate teaches them that speaking up is dangerous, then all you have is a diverse group of people performing unity. 🎭

🎙️ "The question isn't whether your organization has diverse voices. The question is whether it has a system that can actually hear them."

🎙️ The Podcast Question to Sit With...

When an organization becomes quiet — is it becoming peaceful... or is it becoming afraid? ❓
